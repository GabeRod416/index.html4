Are We There Yet?

By Gabriel Rodriguez-Tossas

Dec 24th 2021

Photo from stockvault.net Jack Moreh

The Department of Homeland Security provides data “on foreign nationals who are granted lawful permanent residence (i.e., immigrants who receive a “green card”), admitted as temporary nonimmigrants, granted asylum or refugee status, or are naturalized.” Table 12 of that compendium of tables represents data of where immigrant orphans, which have been adopted by U.S. citizens, are from for the fiscal year of 2019. According to the data provided by the Department of Homeland Security, over half of the orphan children immigrating to the U.S. are from China.

Thanks to the Hague Convention on the Protection of Children and Co-operation in Respect of Intercountry Adoption (Hague Adoption Convention), children from other countries can be adopted by U.S families. These Adopted immigrants children end up traveling and settling into different parts of the country. The Department of State provides data on incoming adoptions by state. According to the 2020 annual report, California and Texas adopted the most children from outside of the country, while Vermont and North Dakota adopted the least.

While the pandemic has been an obstacle for families who are looking to adopt or are waiting for a new member to join their home, access and availability of vaccines have provided some families with hope, as reported by WABI channel 5.
